High Concept

You are heir to a throne. Your brother Alaric was born eleven months behind you and has been eleven months angry ever since; your best friend Gareth is the steward's son who deserves better than court. Across three acts (squire, knight, crown) your choices echo forward, and the game's signature mechanic makes the two fixed NPCs live parallel lives: every climax quest you fail or skip, one of them completes in your place, and the realm quietly starts keeping score.

Design Pillars

  1. Choices echo forward. Small early decisions (who trained you, how you treated your brother on a wall at fifteen) resurface as major consequences acts later.
  2. Parallel lives. Alaric and Gareth have their own mentors, philosophies, and rising reputations. They are shaped by your treatment of them and capable of outshining you.
  3. No single good path. Honor, Ambition, and Cunning open different content, not better content.
  4. Earned extremes. The dramatic endings (Assassinated, Quiet Abdication) are hard-gated behind deliberate multi-act patterns. No accidental tragedy from one bad click.

Act Structure

Act I, The Squire's Trial. Mentor choice; two vignettes (a public mercy dilemma, a rival's humiliation or grace), each with a companion beat; the Scout's Gambit quest; a three-beat pitched battle (formation, save-companion-or-take-glory crisis, mercy / ruthlessness / cleverness toward the beaten lord); knighting epilogue with bond-reactive lines.

Act II, The Knight's Court. A treasury scandal handled by trial, quiet burial, or ownership; three suitors with different costs (alliance that wounds the friend, love match, commoner who trades noble favor for loyalty); the Relic Blade quest; the king's war (council strategy, the father's fall with self / delegate / hold-the-center branches), and the dying king's words, shaped by what you did.

Act III, The Weight of the Crown. Policy decisions with delayed consequences ("The Bill Arrives"); elevate or sideline brother and friend; the Loyalist's Proof quest; the reckoning, which branches into a brother-claimant confrontation (banish / arrest / answer publicly) if bonds curdled, or an external war testing everything banked. Then the ending computation.

The Quest Pickup Mechanic (signature)

Each act has one pre-climax quest. Success improves the player's climax options. Failure or refusal hands the quest to whichever NPC has the higher relevant bond, tie-broken by philosophy fit (covert tasks favor Cunning, martial favor Ambition, political favor Honor). The NPC succeeds in the player's place: their renown rises, flags shift toward them, and in the sharpest case their success feeds the brother's succession claim. If neither NPC fits, the quest fails outright, which is rarer and harsher.

Endings (6)

Threshold-based: Beloved Monarch, Iron Crown, Deposed and Exiled, Died in Battle Legend Born, plus a Steady Crown fallback for middle states. Hard-gated: Assassinated (very negative friend or spouse bond plus a betrayal flag) and Quiet Abdication (a consistent mercy-over-power pattern across all three acts). Companion and spouse epilogues render for every ending.

The Book of Kings

Every completed reign writes an epitaph entry (ending, mentor, marriage, renown, a generated one-line chronicle of key deeds) into persistent storage. The Book is browsable in-game and accumulates across playthroughs, turning replays into an in-world dynasty history.

UX and Presentation

Crimson-and-gold manuscript palette, heraldic SVG crest, staggered text reveal, scene-anchored scrolling, ending title cards. Mobile breakpoints and reduced-motion support throughout.

Balancing Targets

  • First playthrough should end Steady Crown or Iron Crown for most players; Beloved Monarch should require intentional bond investment.
  • Quest player-success odds: 50 percent baseline, +25 for matching mentor philosophy, small renown scaling.
  • The brother-claimant branch should trigger in roughly 1 in 4 careless playthroughs and be fully avoidable with warm bonds.

Production Roadmap (post-POC)

  1. Text depth: double the connective scenes; suitor-specific Act 3 content; more mentor-voiced variants.
  2. Playable siblings: a second campaign as Alaric, reading the same Book of Kings.
  3. Presentation: portrait art, illuminated chapter plates, score.
  4. Book of Kings 2.0: cross-reign callbacks (a past reign's dynasty referenced in a new run's lore).

Risks

Branch cost grows quadratically with reactive text; flags must stay cheap to reference. The pickup mechanic is the pitch and must be impossible to miss when it fires (current build hands the stolen glory a full scene, which is correct). Randomized NPC philosophies add replay texture but complicate authored callbacks; keep philosophy-specific lines modular.
