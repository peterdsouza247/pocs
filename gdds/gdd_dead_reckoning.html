High Concept

You are the commander who never leaves the war room. There is no map to paint and no units to push: the entire war arrives as written field reports from five named officers, and everything you do is choose one of a few costed options per report. The complexity lives in reading the report correctly, because the person writing it has memory, bias, and a trust meter you have been raising or spending for the whole campaign.

The hook: an unreliable-narrator layer on reports (the same world event reads differently depending on the reporting officer's trust) combined with a doctrine-consistency system that rewards the pattern of your decisions, not each decision alone.

Design Pillars

  1. Command through words, not maps. One screen type. Every world event, including things you did not cause, reaches you only as a report. Faction-versus-faction news arrives through Intelligence, delayed and hedged by trust.
  2. Every decision is a bet you can partially read. Qualitative risk bands (Favorable / Risky / Desperate), never raw odds by default. An opt-in Analyst View shows the true modifiers for players who want the math.
  3. Officers fail you differently. Trust is not a flat penalty. At low trust: Voss commits forces without orders; Priya rations silently; Sarai omits the warnings she cannot prove; Cael's idealism curdles into pure realpolitik; Vane runs unsanctioned projects.

Core Loop

Report arrives (officer voice, portrait, theater tag, stakes)
  -> Choose 1 of 2-4 options (visible cost, risk band, sometimes a recommendation)
  -> Hidden roll modified by resources, trust, faction relation, doctrine consistency
  -> Narrated outcome; world state updates
  -> Cycle advances: factions act, consequences from earlier turns surface

Systems

Resources (5)

Materiel, Fuel, Manpower, Intel, Political Capital. Interdependency over quantity: Intel funds the truth of your reports; Political Capital is your leash (at zero, your own government removes you).

Theaters (5)

Secure / Contested / Critical / Lost. Contested fronts drain resources nightly. Three lost theaters ends the campaign.

Officers and trust (0 to 100)

Trust rises by following recommendations and backing officers' calls, falls when overridden. Trust feeds success chances, changes report language (Sarai's hedging tightens into confidence levels at 65+), and gates content (Vane's long-game track opens at high trust). Each officer's low-trust failure mode is a distinct mechanic, listed in pillar 3.

When a failure mode fires, the resulting report is a decision, not just news: the player chooses how to respond (back Voss's initiative or reprimand him; fund Vane's rogue project or shut it down; rebuild Sarai's collection net or note the failure in her file). These reaction choices always resolve as stated and trade trust against Political Capital or resources, so the trust system stays interactive even at its low end.

Doctrine (two axes)

Resolve (Hawk / Dove) and Command Style (Iron Hand / Loose Rein), tracked as a recency-weighted average of invisible option tags. Consistent play earns a quiet resolution bonus; whiplash costs one. Strong positions on both axes earn a named identity (The Hammer, The Fortress, The Warband, The Steward), each with a mechanical signature, announced diegetically. A third Mercy axis is deliberately deferred until these two are proven.

Factions (6)

Sovereign Concord, Skein, Ashpact, Choir, Ledger, Cogent. Each runs learnable logic: doctrine bias, relation to the player, and reaction to the player's visible weakness (the Ashpact raids when your Materiel is low, and an attentive player can reconstruct why). The Cogent is the pacing tool: dormant at start, escalating on a fixed arc (cycles 8, 18, 26) to a finale at cycle 32 with four outcomes shaped by earlier choices (coalition, fortification, or contact).

Career, medals, service record

Permadeath. Success is measured in cycles survived and decorations earned: 10 medals (The Long Watch, Iron Consensus, Quartermaster's Respect, The Silence Broken...). Every campaign archives to a persistent Service Record with ending, identity, medals, and the complete War Journal, reviewable across sessions.

Content Inventory (current build)

6 core report generators (one per officer plus crisis variants), consequence and world-news report types with reaction choices, 4-stage Cogent arc, 3 defeat conditions, 4 finale endings, strategic assessments every 8 cycles, 10 medals, SVG officer portraits, Briefing / Staff / Analyst overlays, full journal scrollback.

UX and Presentation

Terminal-adjacent war room aesthetic: monospace type, amber command text, cyan intelligence, red alarms. One-screen play; War Journal, Staff, Briefing, and Analyst View are optional overlays, never mandatory.

Onboarding and legibility. The HUD is self-describing: labeled rows ("Resources", "Fronts: the five theaters of the war"), theater chips that state their status in words, and hover tooltips on every resource explaining what running out costs. A Command Briefing overlay (main menu and in-game) explains resources, fronts, risk bands, trust, and doctrine in one screen. Every report opens with the officer's SVG portrait, department, and theater tag so the speaker is identifiable at a glance. Confirmed world-news reports carry an exploit-the-window option so informational beats still offer agency.

Balancing Targets

  • Median first campaign: 12 to 18 cycles (death teaches the systems).
  • Experienced campaign reaching the Cogent finale: 32+ cycles.
  • Trust should move roughly 3x slower than resources so relationships feel like investments.
  • No unwinnable state before cycle 5.

Production Roadmap (post-POC)

  1. Report volume: 10x the template pool; multi-report chains with memory ("the sergeant you saved at Meridian writes again").
  2. Officer arcs: personal storylines that pay off trust extremes in both directions, including resignation and replacement officers.
  3. Difficulty and dials: campaign modifiers (early Cogent, hostile start) as unlockables.
  4. Audio: war room ambience, comm-static punctuation, one motif per faction.
  5. Verification: validate the unreliable-narrator hook with market research before full production.

Risks

The game is only as good as its worst-written report; writing is the budget. Players may optimize into ignoring low-trust officers entirely, so low trust must stay dangerous rather than merely quiet. Permadeath plus text density needs a fast first-death experience to avoid onboarding churn.
